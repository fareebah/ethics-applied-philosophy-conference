About the Conference

The Ethics and Applied Philosophy Graduate Conference is a student-organized event oriented toward urgent ethical and political questions. It emphasizes work from marginalized perspectives and aims to make philosophy responsive to contemporary crises.

Perhaps now more than ever, people are being put in crises by modern institutions and social climates that continue to fail and restrict them.

Government officials and systems have become increasingly allied with corporations as their wealthy benefactors, diminishing progress and inflaming systemic injustices; extreme political polarization has divided visions of the world in what often seems like irreconcilable ways; global temperatures are passing a point that would mark the eventual mass extinction of many species across the globe; science and education have lost a worryingly high amount of their public trust and support; international political communities ignore, if not advocate for, the continuation of genocides across the world—most notably against Palestinians; the growth of militarized and violent policing institutions and I.C.E. viciously threaten public wellbeing; and the advent of generative AI and ever-rampant fabrication in news and media have further put conceptions of truth into jeopardy, leaving many to hesitate on what information is good to trust and whether facts even matter anymore.

It is during such troubling times that ethics as a discipline can come into question. We may need to rearticulate canonical ethical theories or reinvent ethics itself in order to properly address these problems philosophically, since ethical practice as it has been traditionally managed may prove difficult or impossible as the current state of our world forces us to cross lines—or, as is often the case, forces lines to cross us—as major, system-wide decisions and actions are made beyond our control.

Now more than ever, we must collectively consider what is within our power as moral agents, how we might best utilize this power to live well and ethically according to our principles, and what the future must look like in order for ethical practice to continue and improve.

Questions: If the canonical theories of Virtue Ethics, Deontology, Consequentialism, Care Ethics, and Non-Ideal Theory are sufficient to understanding and tackling our global crises, do they need to be rearticulated and advocated for in the context of the particular issues that currently plague us? If traditional ethical theories are no longer able to deal with the contemporary world, where might their problem lie? What does the modern crisis reveal as a flaw in the study of ethics thus far? How do we, as moral agents, evaluate ourselves ethically when the conditions of our lives may be controlled by larger powers? What are the implications of this evaluation? What should we as individual moral agents do in the face of major societal and global crises? Is an individual focus for ethics still enough to handle major ethical concerns? How do we respond to violence and harm at a systemic and/or environmental level? Who is morally responsible for this harm, and who is responsible for addressing it? What can (or should) the individual be responsible for in such cases? Where does collective/communal action come into play for living ethically in contemporary times? Who is responsible for collective harm? What kind of collective action can help address it? What is the role of “Truth” in our current social and political climates? How are knowledge and facts wielded, by who, and to what ends? How ought we cultivate good truth-seeking practices to prevent further harm? How can ethics be used to combat apathy and nihilism towards these major crises?
